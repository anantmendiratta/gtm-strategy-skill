# The Nine Growth Motions

Growth is a muscle, not a motion. This file is the deep playbook on each of the nine primary motions — when to pick each, when it fits, when it doesn't, what channels and metrics belong to it, and worked examples.

**The rule: pick ONE primary motion and ONE or TWO supporting motions for Year 1. Everything else is a distraction.**

## 1. Product-Led

**Thesis:** The product is the primary acquisition, activation, and conversion engine. Users try before they buy. The product pulls in more users through use.

**Fits when:**
- ACV is low (<$2,000/yr typical, often <$500/yr)
- Individual user can decide to adopt (no committee)
- Time-to-value is minutes or seconds
- Use is frequent (daily to weekly)
- Product has natural collaboration / sharing / output mechanics
- User can self-onboard without human help

**Doesn't fit when:**
- Enterprise procurement is required
- Time-to-value requires training or setup longer than a day
- The product is used infrequently (quarterly, annually)
- Trust must be established before first use (regulated categories)

**Primary channels:**
- Product virality (collaboration invites, shared output)
- Freemium with clear upgrade trigger
- SEO for templates, integrations, use cases
- Community-driven (templates, gallery, user-generated content)
- Referral mechanics tied to product value (not generic discounts)

**Signal metrics:**
- Activation rate (% of sign-ups reaching first value moment in Day 1)
- Day-30 retention of new cohorts
- Free-to-paid conversion rate
- Viral coefficient (K-factor) or referral rate
- Expansion revenue from existing accounts

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Prove the activation moment works for one specific ICP.
- 1→500: Founders hand-onboard, watch every session, fix every drop-off.
- 500→5K: Product-led acquisition mechanics start compounding.
- 5K+: Sales-assist layer added for expansion / enterprise tier.

**Examples:** Figma, Notion, Slack (early), Loom, Linear, Superhuman (inverted PLG with waitlist).

## 2. Sales-Led

**Thesis:** Humans sell. The product is complex enough, expensive enough, or consequential enough that a buyer needs a person to walk them through it. Marketing generates pipeline; sales closes it.

**Fits when:**
- ACV is high ($10K–$1M+/yr)
- Buying committee exists (champion + economic buyer + IT + legal + procurement)
- Switching cost is significant
- Contract length is annual or multi-year
- Decision has career stakes for the champion
- Product requires configuration, integration, or change management

**Doesn't fit when:**
- ACV is too low to afford sales salaries
- Buyer is individual with immediate need
- Product can be evaluated end-to-end in 30 minutes

**Primary channels:**
- Outbound (SDRs, cold + warm) targeted at specific accounts
- ABM (account-based marketing) against a named account list
- Inbound via category content + lead scoring
- Events and executive roundtables
- Strategic partnerships (channel partners, integrators)
- Analyst relations (Gartner, Forrester, specialist analysts)

**Signal metrics:**
- Pipeline coverage (pipeline value : quota, typically 3–5×)
- SQL-to-Closed Won conversion rate
- Sales cycle length
- Average Contract Value
- Net Revenue Retention (NRR) — should trend to 110%+ in healthy enterprise SaaS
- Win rate by segment / channel

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Founder sells. Every deal is a learning loop on ICP, objection, and price.
- 1→10 customers: Still founder-led, but pattern-matching the ideal deal shape.
- 10–50 customers: First AE hired, playbook documented, SDR experiment.
- 50+: Repeatable sales motion; marketing generates pipeline at predictable CPL.

**Examples:** Salesforce, Palantir, Rippling, Databricks, Snowflake (early), most healthcare enterprise SaaS.

## 3. Story-Led

**Thesis:** A new category, a contrarian POV, or a new way of thinking about the problem pulls the market to you. The founder (or a narrative figure) publishes relentlessly; the audience self-selects; the content becomes the funnel.

**Fits when:**
- You're creating a new category or renaming an old one
- Founder has (or can develop) a distinctive voice
- The buyer Googles / reads / thinks before buying
- Trust is a gating factor (fintech, legal, health, AI)
- Competitors are technically similar; narrative is the differentiator

**Doesn't fit when:**
- The category is mature and commoditized (story won't break through)
- The founder won't/can't publish under their own name
- The buyer is impulse-driven, not research-driven

**Primary channels:**
- Founder's personal brand (LinkedIn, X/Twitter, a blog or newsletter)
- Long-form content (essays, podcast appearances, YouTube)
- PR moments positioned as category stories, not product launches
- Community seeding (becoming the voice in 2–3 communities where the ICP already lives)
- Conference keynotes and curated events

**Signal metrics:**
- Share of voice in the category (mentions, inbound)
- Organic traffic growth (not pageviews — qualified sessions from ICP)
- Newsletter / follower growth (if founder-brand-led)
- Inbound demo / trial requests citing content
- Category search volume trend ("Is the search term for our category growing?")

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Founder publishes 1×/week minimum. The only goal is to find what resonates.
- 1→500: Content generates first cohort. Every post anchored to a constant.
- 500→5K: Predictable inbound from content. One signature moment per quarter.
- 5K+: Brand moves ahead of founder; content team scales what founder seeded.

**Examples:** HubSpot (inbound marketing), Drift (conversational marketing), Notion (productivity-as-creativity), Stripe (developer story), a16z (content-as-distribution).

## 4. Ads-Led

**Thesis:** Paid acquisition is the primary engine. Creative, targeting, and landing page math drive the business. LTV:CAC is the scoreboard.

**Fits when:**
- Unit economics work — LTV:CAC ≥ 3:1, payback ≤ 18 months
- Product category is well-understood (buyer knows the category, just needs your version)
- Creative can be tested and iterated at scale
- Purchase decision is fast or low-stakes enough to happen from an ad
- Targeting is actually possible (ICP is reachable on ad platforms)

**Doesn't fit when:**
- Category is unknown — buyer needs education, not a CTA
- Trust must be established over weeks or months
- ACV is $100K+ (paid ads rarely produce enterprise deals directly)
- Ad platforms cannot target the specific ICP

**Primary channels:**
- Meta / Instagram (DTC, consumer, SMB)
- Google (high-intent, category search)
- YouTube (education + demo, mid-funnel)
- TikTok (Gen Z / Millennial consumer)
- Reddit, X/Twitter ads (niche B2B and SaaS)
- Connected TV / streaming (high-CAC scaling)

**Signal metrics:**
- CAC by channel, by creative
- LTV:CAC (not vanity — actual cohort payback)
- ROAS (return on ad spend) by channel
- Creative turnover rate (how often you ship new creatives)
- Blended CAC vs. paid CAC
- First-order profitability (can you be profitable on day 1 on a single purchase?)

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Test 3–5 channels with small budgets, find the one that doesn't immediately lose money.
- 1→500: Double down on the winner; iterate creative weekly.
- 500→5K: Build a creative engine (5–20 new creatives/week). Begin diversifying channels.
- 5K+: Blended paid + content + retention compound; attribution shifts to MTA/MMM.

**Examples:** Most DTC brands (Dollar Shave Club in paid-video era, Allbirds early, Gymshark), mobile gaming, a lot of consumer fintech (Acorns, Robinhood early-paid phases).

## 5. Creative-Led

**Thesis:** Distinctive brand and creative is the moat. The product may be commodity; the story and the aesthetic are what people pay for. Attention is the scarce resource; creative is the extraction tool.

**Fits when:**
- Category is commoditized or crowded; product differences are small
- Emotional / identity purchase (apparel, beverage, lifestyle, beauty)
- Brand can be built around a distinctive worldview or attitude
- Founder has or can develop real creative taste
- Budget exists for distinctive creative work (not necessarily big — just distinctive)

**Doesn't fit when:**
- Buyer is purely rational (B2B tooling often falls here)
- Product is genuinely technically differentiated (lead with that instead)
- No one on the team has creative conviction — creative-led requires taste

**Primary channels:**
- Organic social (IG, TikTok, YouTube) — creative is the content
- PR + earned media around distinctive campaigns
- OOH (outdoor) in targeted cities for brand-building
- Collaborations and cultural moments
- Paid social — but creative-first, not performance-first
- Retail presence as creative expression (packaging, store design)

**Signal metrics:**
- Aided and unaided brand awareness in ICP
- Earned media impressions and sentiment
- Direct / branded search volume trend
- Organic social engagement (creative-fit signal)
- Repeat purchase rate (does the brand outlast the ad?)
- Gross margin that supports creative investment

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Nail the one thing the brand stands for. One point of view, sharply stated.
- 1→500: Signature creative moments that signal the brand's worldview.
- 500→5K: Consistent creative system; start scaling with paid behind the strongest creatives.
- 5K+: Brand becomes cultural reference; earned media compounds.

**Examples:** Liquid Death, Oatly, Duolingo, Aesop, Nike (forever), Glossier (also channel-led via IG).

## 6. Model-Led

**Thesis:** The business or pricing model is the innovation. The product is similar to what exists; the way it's packaged, priced, delivered, or financed is what unlocks demand.

**Fits when:**
- The underlying service or product already exists but is badly packaged
- Friction, gatekeeping, or pricing structure is what's keeping customers out
- There's a new technology or cost structure that lets you restructure the model
- You can sustain the unusual model long enough for the market to catch up

**Doesn't fit when:**
- The model innovation is trivial or easy to copy (incumbents will respond)
- You can't explain the model in one sentence

**Examples of model innovation:**
- Subscription instead of one-time (Netflix, Dollar Shave Club, SaaS itself)
- Marketplace instead of inventory (Airbnb, Uber)
- Pay-per-use instead of flat fee (AWS, Stripe, usage-based SaaS)
- Vertical integration instead of middleman (Warby Parker, Casper, Tesla)
- Free / ad-supported instead of paid (Facebook, Spotify free tier)
- Outcome-based instead of time-based (some marketing agencies, some services)
- Bundle instead of à la carte (MoviePass — also cautionary), or the reverse (unbundling cable)

**Primary channels:**
- Whichever channel best demonstrates the model's advantage
- PR / earned media about the model itself (the model IS the story)
- Comparison content ("the old way vs. the new way")
- Trials designed to expose the model's advantage quickly

**Signal metrics:**
- Unit economics that prove the model works (not just at launch, at scale)
- Cohort LTV and retention
- Conversion from "surprised by the model" to "convinced"
- NPS / referral among users who "get" the model

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Prove the model isn't a gimmick — actual unit economics work.
- 1→500: Communicate the model clearly; build content that teaches the shift.
- 500→5K: Model-based PR moments; category reframing.
- 5K+: Incumbents either copy or cede the category.

**Examples:** Airbnb, Stripe, Warby Parker, Robinhood (commission-free), Netflix (streaming + later subscription-only), Tesla (direct sales + OTA updates).

## 7. Channel-Led

**Thesis:** Dominate one channel where competitors are absent or weak. The channel is not just an acquisition source — it's a moat. Your product is designed to ride this channel better than anyone else can.

**Fits when:**
- One platform or channel has disproportionate concentration of your ICP
- You have an unfair advantage on that channel (audience, content expertise, first-mover, format mastery)
- Your product generates content or output native to the channel
- Competitors are spread across channels; you focus on one

**Doesn't fit when:**
- The channel's algorithm or policy could change and wipe you out (platform risk)
- Your ICP is fragmented across many channels
- You don't have meaningful depth on the channel (don't go Channel-Led on a channel you don't understand)

**Primary channels (pick one as the dominant):**
- Instagram (Glossier, most beauty DTC)
- TikTok (Scrub Daddy renaissance, many Gen-Z brands)
- YouTube (MrBeast Feastables, most creator-led brands)
- SEO (Notion templates, Canva templates, Zapier integration pages)
- Reddit / niche forums (some developer tools)
- LinkedIn (many B2B prosumer tools)
- App store ASO (mobile-first businesses)
- Amazon (some DTC brands that pivoted Amazon-first)
- Specific podcasts (niche B2B advisory, some finance products)
- Physical retail placement (Warby Parker in Nordstrom, some CPG)

**Signal metrics:**
- Share of voice on the chosen channel
- CPA on the channel vs. alternatives
- Channel-specific engagement (CTR, watch time, follow rate)
- % of new users from the dominant channel (should be >60% for Year 1 to be truly channel-led)
- Channel-driven cohort retention vs. other channels

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Learn the channel deeply. Post/publish/optimize 5× what you think is necessary.
- 1→500: First cohort from the channel. Iterate on format and hook.
- 500→5K: Systematize. Hire / train for channel depth.
- 5K+: Use channel dominance as leverage for other channels (cross-channel halo).

**Examples:** Glossier (Instagram), Notion (template SEO), Dollar Shave Club (YouTube launch video → PR), Morning Brew (LinkedIn + newsletter), many Substacks.

## 8. Data-Led

**Thesis:** The product improves with usage. More users → more data → better product → more users. The data flywheel is the core growth engine; acquisition serves the flywheel.

**Fits when:**
- Product quality improves measurably with scale (recommendations, rankings, fraud detection, ML models)
- Network effects or data advantages compound over time
- Usage creates a proprietary dataset a competitor can't replicate
- You can afford a period of sub-par product quality while data accumulates

**Doesn't fit when:**
- Product is static (data doesn't improve it meaningfully)
- Competitors have larger data sources already
- Regulatory constraints block data accumulation

**Primary channels:**
- Whichever channel gets usage fastest (often starts as PLG or Sales-Led)
- Free tier or loss-leader to accelerate data generation
- Strategic partnerships that feed unique data
- Community-generated data (UGC, reviews, ratings)

**Signal metrics:**
- Data quality improvements (precision/recall, error rate) as usage grows
- Cohort performance: do later cohorts get better results than earlier ones?
- Feedback loop latency (how fast does new data improve the product?)
- Unique data assets accumulated

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Make the flywheel real (even at tiny scale) — cohort 2 should perform measurably better than cohort 1.
- 1→500: Prove the slope. Usage doubles, quality jumps.
- 500→5K: The data moat becomes a marketing claim backed by numbers.
- 5K+: Competitors can't catch up on data; you monetize the moat.

**Examples:** Google, TikTok, Netflix, Scale AI, Perplexity, Bloomberg Terminal, Stripe Radar (fraud), Ramp (spend data).

## 9. Event/Community-Led

**Thesis:** Trust and adoption travel through faces, rooms, and groups — not ads or content. The company organizes or shows up where the ICP gathers, and the relationships become the funnel.

**Fits when:**
- ICP gathers in person (conferences, local meetups, industry events, trade shows)
- Trust in the category is built through peer-to-peer, not broadcast
- The product is consequential enough that buyers want human signal before buying
- Category is regulated / professional (medical, legal, finance, trades)
- Geographic or local markets (local services, local SaaS, regional brands)

**Doesn't fit when:**
- ICP is globally distributed, remote-first, online-native (PLG / story-led often wins instead)
- Events cost more per lead than the ACV can support
- No one on the team can run events — they're hard

**Primary channels:**
- Curated small dinners and roundtables (often highest ROI for enterprise)
- Industry conferences (sponsorship or speaking slot)
- Your own conference or summit (HubSpot's INBOUND, Salesforce's Dreamforce, Figma's Config)
- Local meetups and chapters
- Private communities (Slack, Discord, or even Telegram groups for specific ICPs)
- Creator-led or advisor-led communities (Lenny's, Exec, On Deck, Reforge)
- Field sales with local relationship density

**Signal metrics:**
- Pipeline sourced from events (measured 60–90 days post-event)
- Community engagement (weekly active members, posts, replies)
- Referral rate from community members
- Cost per closed deal via events
- Retention of event-sourced vs. ads-sourced users (events usually retain better)

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: 3–5 dinners with the 15 most respected people in the category. No pitch — just relationships and context.
- 1→500: Show up at 3–5 industry events as a speaker or contributor. Host one of your own.
- 500→5K: Your conference, your community, your category.
- 5K+: Event becomes a category-defining institution.

**Examples:** Salesforce (Dreamforce), HubSpot (INBOUND), Figma (Config), Notion (ambassador program + events), Lenny's Newsletter (community), most enterprise B2B, many regional / local businesses.

## 10. Integration-Led

**Thesis:** The product doesn't ask users to change their workflow — it moves into the workflow they already use. The distribution channel is the integration itself. Adoption happens because the product appears inside the tool the user already opens every morning.

**Fits when:**
- Your ICP lives in a specific platform all day (developers in their IDE; sales teams in Salesforce; ops teams in Slack or Notion; lawyers in document editors)
- The product's value is contextual — it's more useful in the place where the work happens than as a separate destination
- The host platform has a marketplace or extension ecosystem you can publish into
- You have a B2B2C dynamic: enterprises buy your product to deploy inside their existing tooling

**Doesn't fit when:**
- The workflow is genuinely new (users have no platform to embed into — you must create the destination)
- The host platform competes with you or could build you natively
- Platform dependency risk is prohibitive for your enterprise buyers (common in regulated industries)
- Your product's value requires long sessions of focused work that don't happen inside existing tools

**Primary channels:**
- App store / marketplace of the host platform (Slack App Directory, VS Code Marketplace, Salesforce AppExchange, Notion marketplace, Chrome Web Store)
- Developer community of the host platform (their Discord, their dev docs, their GitHub)
- Partner co-marketing with the host platform (often available once you reach volume)
- Content targeted at users of the host platform ("best Salesforce tools for X" → you appear)
- Sales motion: show up in the renewal conversation for the host platform ("already in your stack")

**Signal metrics:**
- Integration installs / activations (not just downloads)
- Activation rate within the host platform context (different from standalone activation)
- Daily active use within the host platform context
- Expansion: how many users in an account activate after the first user
- Retention: are users staying when the host platform renews?

**Stage sequence:**
- 0→1: Build one deep integration for one specific platform. Don't try to be on every marketplace at once.
- 1→500: Optimize activation within the host context. The user's first session in your integration is the entire funnel.
- 500→5K: Get featured by the host platform. Co-market at their conference. Become a named partner.
- 5K+: Multi-platform expansion, or build the destination product on top of the integration's installed base.

**AI-era relevance:** Integration-Led is the dominant growth pattern for AI tools in 2024–2026. The reason: users are fatigued by standalone AI apps. They don't want to open another tab. AI products that embed into Slack, VS Code, Salesforce, Google Workspace, or Notion absorb into existing behavior instead of asking for behavioral change. For AI products, the integration IS the trust ladder — users experience the AI in a familiar context, which dramatically reduces adoption friction.

**Examples:** GitHub Copilot (VS Code, JetBrains), Notion AI (inside Notion), Salesforce Einstein (inside Salesforce), Harvey (inside document workflows), Grammarly (inside browser), Superhuman (inside email), Loom (inside Slack/Notion embeds), Figma plugins (inside Figma).

## Selection Decision Tree

Use this as a first-pass filter. Validate by reading the relevant motion's section above.

```
1. Is ACV > $10K/yr AND committee buyer?
   → Yes: SALES-LED primary. Story-Led supports (category/inbound feeds pipeline).
   → No: continue.

2. Is the product used daily AND does it have natural sharing/collab mechanics?
   → Yes: PRODUCT-LED primary. Channel-Led or Story-Led supports.
   → No: continue.

3. Are you creating a new category OR does the founder have distinctive voice + willingness to publish?
   → Yes: STORY-LED primary. Motion 1 or 2 supports depending on ACV.
   → No: continue.

4. Is category commoditized / crowded AND the opportunity is a distinctive worldview?
   → Yes: CREATIVE-LED primary. Channel-Led (IG / TikTok / YouTube) or Ads-Led supports.
   → No: continue.

5. Is the business-model innovation the actual wedge?
   → Yes: MODEL-LED primary. Story-Led supports.
   → No: continue.

6. Is there ONE channel where your ICP is 5–10× more concentrated than elsewhere?
   → Yes: CHANNEL-LED primary. Supporting motion depends on channel.
   → No: continue.

7. Does the product measurably improve with usage data AND network effects compound?
   → Yes: DATA-LED primary. PLG or Sales-Led usually supports (whichever gets usage fastest).
   → No: continue.

8. Does the ICP gather in person / in community more than online? OR is the category trust-heavy and regulated?
   → Yes: EVENT/COMMUNITY-LED primary. Story-Led usually supports.
   → No: continue.

9. Does the product's value happen inside a tool the user already opens every day (IDE, Slack, Salesforce, Notion, browser)?
   → Yes: INTEGRATION-LED primary. Story-Led (for awareness) or PLG (for in-platform activation) supports.
   → No: continue.

10. Default for consumer / DTC / mobile app with working unit economics:
    → ADS-LED primary. Creative-Led or Channel-Led supports.
```

**Trust-heavy override:** Regardless of the path above, if the category is legal, medical, financial, compliance, enterprise security, or anything career-defining for the buyer — Story-Led and Event/Community-Led tend to dominate in Year 1 because trust must be proven before any other motion has access to the buyer.

**Founder-asymmetry override:** If the founder has a pre-existing distribution advantage (large audience, ex-operator with network, rare category credibility), often the right move is to ride that advantage — even if the "right" motion per the tree above is different. Founder-market fit matters.

**AI-product override:** If the product is an AI tool, ask: does the value happen *somewhere the user already is* (Integration-Led) or does it require a new destination? AI tools with a clear host platform should default to Integration-Led (or add it as a strong supporting motion) because integration dramatically reduces adoption friction and resets the trust calculus — users experience AI in a familiar context rather than a strange new app.
