# The Four Fits Diagnostic

Brian Balfour's Four Fits Framework — adapted as a diagnostic you run on any business before prescribing a GTM.

**Source:** Brian Balfour, *The Four Fits* (brianbalfour.com/four-fits-growth-framework)

**Core insight:** You need all four fits to grow to meaningful scale. They are an ecosystem — broken fits cannot be compensated by strong ones. Changing one (e.g., moving upmarket, cutting ACV, switching channels) forces re-diagnosing the others.

---

## The Four Fits

### 1. Market–Product Fit

**Question:** Does the target market actually have the problem you're solving, and does your specific product solve it for them — not for a different adjacent segment?

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- Name the specific user, not a persona. What role, geography, company size, life stage?
- What do they do today when your product doesn't exist? (If "nothing" — the problem isn't acute enough.)
- Would they pay for it in cash today? Or only if it's free?
- Have you validated this qualitatively (interviews), quantitatively (conversion data, retention), AND intuitively (does this feel like a real pain)?

**Working signals:** Early users rave, retain, refer without prompting. Specific language keeps recurring in user interviews.

**Broken signals:** Users sign up then never return. Feedback is polite, not urgent. "It's nice but I wouldn't miss it if it went away."

**If broken:** Narrow the ICP. Go smaller, more specific. Kill features. Talk to 20 users in the next 2 weeks. Don't touch channels yet — no channel can save a product that doesn't solve a real problem for a specific person.

---

### 2. Product–Channel Fit

**Question:** Is the product designed to ride the channels you plan to use? Products are built to fit channels — channels do not mold to products.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- If you say "paid ads" — does the product have an ad-friendly hook (visual, emotional, fast time-to-value) and economics that support CAC?
- If you say "SEO" — does the product generate shareable content, templates, or answers at scale?
- If you say "virality / PLG" — is there a natural reason for a user to pull another user in (collaboration, sharing output, network effect)?
- If you say "sales-led" — is the ACV high enough to afford a salesperson? Is the buying committee identifiable?
- If you say "community / events" — does the product create visible wins that users want to show off in community?

**Working signals:** The channel mechanics are designed into the product, not bolted on after. Users invite other users because the product works better with them. Content generates itself from product usage.

**Broken signals:** The growth team is "trying every channel" with no clear winner. Paid ads are the only thing that works but LTV/CAC is upside down. The product has zero natural reason for users to share it.

**If broken:** Either redesign the product to fit the channel (add collaboration, add shareability, add content output) or switch to a channel the product was actually designed for. Don't force a product into a channel it wasn't built for.

---

### 3. Channel–Model Fit

**Question:** Do your channel costs and your revenue model mathematically work together? ARPU and CAC must live in the same economic zone.

**The ARPU↔CAC spectrum (Balfour):**

| ARPU / year | Viable channels | Examples |
|-------------|----------------|----------|
| $0 – $100 | Virality, SEO, content, PLG, community, ads at massive scale | Consumer apps, DTC under $100, freemium SaaS |
| $100 – $1,000 | Content marketing, SEO, PLG with a sales assist, targeted paid | SMB SaaS, prosumer tools |
| $1,000 – $10,000 | Inside sales, content + sales assist, events, partnerships | Mid-market SaaS |
| $10,000 – $100,000 | Inside/outside sales, ABM, events, strategic partnerships | Mid-market to enterprise |
| $100,000+ | Enterprise sales, exec relationships, strategic accounts, field sales | Enterprise, deep tech |

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- What's your current (or planned) ARPU?
- What do your planned channels typically cost in CAC for this category? (Paid social ~$30–300 per acquisition consumer; ABM $1K–5K per SQL B2B; SDR ~$5K–15K per deal mid-market.)
- Does LTV:CAC land at 3:1 or better within a reasonable payback period (12–18 months)?
- Are you trying to sell a $200 product using a $2,000 sales motion? Or trying to sell a $200,000 product via a Facebook ad?

**Working signals:** The math works unit-by-unit at current scale and still works projected at 10× scale.

**Broken signals:** Each sale takes more human time than the revenue justifies. Paid ads are "working" but you're losing money per user and hoping LTV will catch up.

**If broken:** Either move ARPU (raise price, package higher, go upmarket) or move channels (switch to a cheaper acquisition motion). Don't try to brute-force a channel the math won't support.

---

### 4. Model–Market Fit

**Question:** Is the market large enough to support a meaningful business at your ARPU and realistic capture rate?

**The equation:** *ARPU × Total Customers in Market × Realistic Capture %* = your revenue ceiling.

**Diagnostic prompts:**
- How many potential customers exist? (Specific count or realistic range.)
- At your ARPU, what revenue does 1% capture produce? 5%? 10%?
- Is the market growing, flat, or shrinking?
- Are you a $100M business at your current model, or a $10M lifestyle business? Both are fine — but be honest.

**Working signals:** 1–5% market capture at current ARPU clears $100M. Market is growing.

**Broken signals:** Even 30% capture at current ARPU doesn't clear $20M. You need to raise price, expand scope, go multi-product, or accept a smaller business.

**If broken:** Three options — (1) raise ARPU significantly (usually by moving upmarket or adding products), (2) expand TAM (new segments, new geographies), or (3) accept that this is a smaller business and size the ambition accordingly.

## How to Use the Diagnostic

**Output a table in the GTM document:**

| Fit | Status | Evidence | Implication for Year 1 |
|-----|--------|----------|----------------------|
| Market–Product | Working / Unknown / Broken | Specific evidence or assumption | What Year 1 must prove |
| Product–Channel | … | … | … |
| Channel–Model | … | … | … |
| Model–Market | … | … | … |

**The broken or unknown fit is the thing Year 1 must prove.** That becomes the Q1 or Q2 objective. Don't try to "scale" until the broken fit is fixed — you'll just scale a leaky bucket.

**Common patterns:**

- **Early-stage startup with MVP:** Market–Product is usually Unknown. Year 1 must prove it with a small focused cohort before spending on channels.
- **Product works but growth is stalled:** Product–Channel is usually broken. Either redesign for a channel or switch channels.
- **Losing money per user but "growing":** Channel–Model is broken. Unit economics must be fixed before more fuel is added.
- **Great product, great growth, small market:** Model–Market is the ceiling. Multi-product or upmarket move is the next fit to solve.

**Revisit the diagnostic every 6 months.** All four fits drift as the company grows. The fit that got you to $1M ARR is not the fit that gets you to $10M.

## AI Era Overlays

AI products interact with the Four Fits in ways that didn't exist before. Apply these overlays when the product's core value proposition involves an AI model.

### Market–Product Fit (AI)

The novelty problem: AI products have a "wow moment" that drives early sign-ups but doesn't equal retained value. Early enthusiasm masks weak PMF.

**Additional diagnostic prompts:**
- Is the AI actually needed, or is the value in the workflow wrapped around it? (Remove the AI; what remains? If users would still pay for the workflow, the AI is a feature. If the whole value collapses, AI is load-bearing.)
- What happens after the novelty wears off at Day 30? (AI products that solve real problems retain. AI products that are impressive demonstrations don't.)
- Is the AI reliably right enough that users trust it with real work — or do they still need to verify every output? (Reliability is the actual PMF signal for trust-heavy AI. Not accuracy in demos — accuracy when it matters.)
- Would users miss it if it went away, or would they switch to GPT-4 directly for the same outcome?

**AI-specific broken signal:** Sign-ups are strong. Day-7 is reasonable. Day-30 falls off a cliff. Users say "it's impressive but I'm not sure I need it every day." This is novelty PMF, not real PMF. Narrow the ICP and the use case before spending on channels.

---

### Product–Channel Fit (AI)

AI creates new product-native channels that didn't exist pre-2022:

- **AI tool directories** (There's An AI For That, Futurepedia, Product Hunt AI, etc.) — high intent, but quality of traffic decays fast as directories fill up. Treat as launch moment, not steady-state channel.
- **LLM marketplaces** (Claude App Store, OpenAI GPT Store, Anthropic Marketplace) — platform risk is real (the platform can build your product natively) but can be a high-quality early acquisition channel.
- **Integration ecosystems** (Slack marketplace, Notion marketplace, VS Code extensions, Salesforce AppExchange) — this is Integration-Led growth; if the product lives inside a tool users already use daily, the channel is the integration itself.
- **AI demo as sales motion** — a live AI demo compresses trust-building in ways no deck can. For sales-led AI products, the demo is the channel. Invest in making it flawless and replicable before anything else.
- **Benchmark/eval publishing** — for developer tools and enterprise AI, publishing your benchmarks is content marketing. "We score X on Y benchmark" drives qualified inbound from buyers who care.

**Additional diagnostic prompts:**
- Is the product API-first? If yes, developer-led growth (GitHub, HuggingFace, dev communities) should be in the mix.
- Does the product live inside the user's existing workflow (in their IDE, their Slack, their CRM) or does it require them to go somewhere new? The latter has much higher activation friction.
- Is the AI demo something a rep can replicate reliably — or does the AI sometimes fail in front of a prospect, damaging trust? (Brittle demos are a channel problem, not just a product problem.)

---

### Channel–Model Fit (AI)

AI adds a new cost line that most frameworks ignore: **inference cost**. A $30/user/month SaaS product with $8/user/month inference is a 73% gross margin business — not the 80%+ SaaS benchmark. This changes channel math.

**Additional diagnostic prompts:**
- What's the inference cost per active user per month, at current usage and projected peak usage?
- At scale, does the margin structure still support the planned acquisition channels? (A 50% gross margin AI product cannot run the same paid acquisition math as an 80% margin SaaS.)
- Is pricing usage-based (tokens, calls, seats × usage)? If so, the CAC equation has a new variable: the cost-to-serve compounds with usage, which is both the opportunity and the risk.
- Does the free tier exist — and if so, what's the cost of a free user? AI free tiers can be structurally loss-making in ways that traditional SaaS freemium is not.

**The ARPU↔CAC spectrum for AI:**

| Gross Margin (after inference) | Channel implication |
|-------------------------------|---------------------|
| > 75% | Standard SaaS channel math applies |
| 60–75% | PLG and low-CAC channels are fine; SDRs need careful unit economics |
| 40–60% | Must price higher or find near-zero CAC channels (virality, integration, community); paid acquisition math breaks |
| < 40% | Channel–Model fit is broken. Raise price, reduce inference cost, or move model provider. |

---

### Model–Market Fit (AI)

AI markets move faster. What's a new category in Q1 can be a commodity by Q4. The Model–Market fit question requires a 3-year view, not just "is the market large enough today."

**Additional diagnostic prompts:**
- What is your moat when the underlying model commoditizes? Name it specifically — data flywheel, workflow embedding, network effects, proprietary dataset, or switching cost. "We use a better model" is not a moat.
- **The commoditization pressure test:** Would your business survive if OpenAI/Google/Anthropic built your core feature natively into their product? If no — what would need to be true to change the answer? (This is the single most important question for AI product strategy.)
- Is the market growing because AI is enabling a new behavior (durable), or because AI is novel and people are exploring (temporary)? The former compounds; the latter decays.
- Are you competing with the model itself? (An AI writing tool competes with ChatGPT directly. A legal workflow automation that uses AI does not — it competes with the old workflow.) The former is a much harder position.

**Revisit the diagnostic every quarter for AI businesses**, not every 6 months. Model releases from OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, and Meta can shift Market–Product fit and Product–Channel fit faster than any other technology era has experienced.
