# Objection Reframing

Every founder will face 3 recurring objections from investors, customers, or category skeptics. Get ahead of them.

**The single rule: never defend the technology. Always reframe to one of four moats — Moat, Data, Workflow, or Category.**

- **Moat:** The thing that makes you defensible long-term — proprietary data, network effects, switching cost, regulatory edge.
- **Data:** The flywheel of usage → data → better product → more usage.
- **Workflow:** The fact that you are embedded in daily/weekly workflow of the user, not a sidecar.
- **Category:** You are defining a new category; being compared to incumbents is a category mistake.

---

## Output Format for Each Objection

Always produce two versions — the full response (pitch, demo, interview) and the one-liner (5-minute conversation, elevator, casual ask).

```
### Objection: [Exact phrasing the founder will hear]

**Full response** (pitch, demo, interview):
[3–5 sentence reframe. Land the moat / data / workflow / category point. Include one specific proof point if possible — a number, a named customer, a concrete example.]

**One-liner** (casual ask, 5-min conversation):
[One sentence. Quotable. Memorable. Passes the "could the founder text this" test.]
```

## Common Objections and Reframe Patterns

### "You're just a wrapper" (AI, API, or platform wrapper)

**Reframe to:** Workflow + Data.

**Full:** "The model is a commodity. What we own is the workflow a [specific user] actually uses every day, and the data we generate from that usage — which the foundation model provider doesn't see. A wrapper is a chat box over an API. We're the system of record for how [specific user] actually does [specific workflow], with [specific proprietary data type] that compounds with every customer we add."

**One-liner:** "We're not wrapping the model. We're owning the workflow and the data the model will never see."

---

### "There are 10 tools like this" / "The space is crowded"

**Reframe to:** Category + ICP Specificity.

**Full:** "You're describing a category; we're describing an ICP. The tools you're naming serve [adjacent buyer or use case]. We serve [specific buyer], for whom none of those tools are actually designed. Our Day-90 retention with that ICP is [X%] — which is the number that matters, not the feature overlap."

**One-liner:** "Ten tools share our category. None of them have our customer."

---

### "This is a feature, not a company"

**Reframe to:** Category + Workflow.

**Full:** "That framing assumes we're competing with an existing category. We're defining a new one — [category name]. If this were a feature, [incumbent] would have built it. They haven't, because the workflow we're owning isn't the workflow they're organized around. We intend to be the system of record for [specific category], and 'feature' becomes 'platform' the moment a standalone company reaches [specific scale]."

**One-liner:** "It's only a feature if the incumbent was going to build it. They're not."

---

### "Why would anyone switch from [incumbent]?"

**Reframe to:** Workflow + Specific Pain.

**Full:** "They don't switch because we're better in the abstract. They switch because [incumbent] was built for [old buyer / old workflow] and our user is [new buyer / new workflow] that [incumbent] doesn't serve natively. Three of our [first customers] told us the same thing — [specific pain quote]. We're not competing on features; we're competing on whether [incumbent] was ever really built for this user in the first place."

**One-liner:** "They don't switch for features. They switch because the incumbent was never built for them."

---

### "The market is too small"

**Reframe to:** Model-Market Fit shift.

**Full:** "It's too small at [current positioning]. At [new positioning or model], the TAM is [larger number]. But also — we're not trying to capture a market; we're trying to build a category that pulls adjacent buyers in as it matures. [Specific adjacent segment] represents 3× the volume of the initial ICP, and our product is being used by [early sign] of that segment already."

**One-liner:** "It's small if you count today's users. Categories aren't sized by where they start."

---

### "Your CAC is too high" / "Unit economics don't work"

**Reframe to:** Stage + Channel-Model Fit trajectory.

**Full:** "Paid is not the acquisition engine at this stage. Our Q1 unit economics are [the current reality] because we're intentionally in the trust-building phase. The channels we're building — [founder brand / community / referral / in-product virality] — have marginal CAC approaching zero as they compound. By Q3, our blended CAC will be [projection]. We've chosen a more patient channel mix on purpose."

**One-liner:** "We're not buying users. We're building the channels that will bring them for free."

---

### "What's stopping [big incumbent] from building this?"

**Reframe to:** Workflow + Customer Focus.

**Full:** "Three reasons. One: their organizational structure is built around [their current workflow], not ours — building this would mean restructuring teams, not shipping a feature. Two: even if they built it, their sales motion is optimized for [big enterprise], not [our ICP] — they'd compete with themselves. Three: our user doesn't trust [incumbent] for this specific problem because [specific reason]. Trust is the actual moat here, not code."

**One-liner:** "They could build it. They won't, because it would compete with their core business — and even if they did, our user doesn't trust them for it."

---

### "Who's your customer — really?"

**Reframe to:** Specificity + Proof.

**Full:** "Our customer is [very specific role + geography + context], and here's how I know: [specific user story — name or number]. We've talked to [N] of them; [N2] are paying; Day-30 retention is [X%]. The 'serious' filter is [specific behavior we look for]. We're not building for 'everyone who could use this' — we're building for the [specific user] who [specific pain], and we've learned to say no to the rest."

**One-liner:** "Our customer is [very specific user] — and the reason we know is [specific retention or revenue proof]."

---

### "This will commoditize" (for AI / tech products)

**Reframe to:** Data Flywheel + Switching Cost.

**Full:** "Models will commoditize. Distribution won't. Data won't. Workflow embedding won't. The reason we chose [specific vertical / specific workflow] is precisely because the commoditization of the model layer is the best thing that can happen to us — we don't differentiate on model quality. We differentiate on the [specific proprietary data / workflow / trust] that compounds every month we operate."

**One-liner:** "Model commoditization is a gift. Our moat was never the model."

---

### "What happens when OpenAI / Google / Anthropic ships this natively?"

**Reframe to:** Workflow + Data + Switching Cost.

**Full:** "That's the right question to ask. Here's our honest answer: the model providers compete on general capability — we compete on [specific vertical workflow / specific user type / specific data]. When they build a general writing tool, we own [contracts for M&A lawyers in India]. When they build a general coding assistant, we own [the deployment workflow for mid-market DevOps teams]. The surface area they can natively serve is horizontal; the surface area worth owning is vertical. Our [specific data / specific workflow / specific integration] creates a switching cost they don't create — and the better their foundation model gets, the better our product gets without us doing anything."

**One-liner:** "Their job is to build the general model. Our job is to own the vertical where it gets applied — and we have a [year / months / N customers] head start on that."

---

### "We're worried about hallucinations / accuracy"

**Reframe to:** Workflow + Trust Architecture.

**Full:** "This is a real concern — and it's why we built the verification step directly into the workflow. The AI doesn't replace judgment; it handles [specific task] and flags where confidence is low. In our current deployment with [N] customers, the error rate on [specific task] is [X%] — lower than the [existing manual process] it replaces. Trust isn't a function of whether the AI is perfect; it's a function of whether the overall system, with humans in the right places, produces better outcomes than what you had before."

**One-liner:** "We didn't build an AI that's always right. We built a system where being wrong has a safety net — and the overall accuracy beats the process it replaces."

---

### "Why now?"

**Reframe to:** Constants + Unlock.

**Full:** "The constants behind this business — [name your 3 constants] — have always existed. What changed now is [specific unlock: cost curve, regulatory shift, behavior change, adjacent tech maturity]. Without that unlock, this product wasn't possible; with it, it's inevitable. We're not riding a trend; we're catching a permanent human want that a new capability has finally made serveable."

**One-liner:** "The need has always been there. [Specific unlock] is what made it finally serveable."

---

## How to Pick the 3 Objections to Address

Not every objection is worth pre-empting. Pick the 3 that:

1. **The founder will hear in every pitch / every sales call.** Pattern over a handful of conversations is enough.
2. **The category itself invites.** AI wrapper objection is near-universal for AI products. "Feature not company" is near-universal for adjacent-to-incumbent plays.
3. **The founder fumbles currently.** The answer isn't reflexive yet. Objections they already answer well don't need this treatment.

**Don't include objections the founder isn't actually getting.** The document should be grounded, not theoretical.

---

## The Anti-Pattern: Defending the Technology

**Never:** "But our RAG pipeline is better because we use [technique]."

The buyer doesn't care about the technique. They care about whether their life is better, whether they can trust you, and whether the decision will look good 12 months from now.

Every technology-defense answer is a failed reframe. Catch yourself when you're explaining *how it works* instead of *why it matters*.
